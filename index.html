Techniques Used

Email Manager

01
Connected Gmail via MCP
Used the Gmail MCP connector in Cowork to give Claude direct access to my inbox. No copy-paste, no manual exports — it reads my actual emails in real time.
02
Invoked the email-manager skill
The skill contains instructions for how to triage: what counts as urgent, how to categorize threads, what to draft vs. just flag. It reads those instructions on every run so I never have to re-explain the system.
03
Layered in the humanize-writing skill
For any email that needs a draft reply, Claude uses the humanize-writing skill to strip AI tells and make it sound like me — not like a language model filling in a template.
04
Referenced my Email Voice Guide for tone
Every draft is calibrated against my voice guide: how I open emails, how formal I am with different audiences, my sentence patterns, and what I never say. The result actually sounds like me.
05
Receives a triage summary + ready-to-send drafts
Output is a structured digest: what needs my attention, what's already drafted in Gmail, and what's been filed. I review and send — the cognitive load is almost zero.

What Makes This Different
  • Voice-matched replies — not generic AI drafts. My voice guide makes the difference.
  • Triage logic is in the skill — no re-prompting. The skill holds the rules.
  • Gmail MCP = real access — reads live emails, saves drafts directly. No clipboard gymnastics.
  • Layered skills — email-manager + humanize-writing working together. Each does one thing well.

The 8 Sections of the Voice Guide

Analyzed 40 sent emails. AI identified my default tone, how I adapt across contexts, and the patterns I repeat without thinking. Output: a structured reference doc any AI can use to write emails that sound like me.

Section 01
Core Identity
My default tone, the register I fall into when I'm not thinking about it, and what I value in communication.
Section 02
Registers
How I shift across contexts — with a boss, a peer, a recruiter, a client. What changes and what stays constant.
Section 03
Sentence Patterns
How I open, how I close, sentence length, rhythm, how I punctuate — the mechanical fingerprint of my writing.
Section 04
Rhetorical Moves
The persuasion tactics I reach for most — framing, bridging, the moves I make to be heard without being pushy.
Section 05
Emotional Register
Where I'm warm vs. professional, when I inject humor, how I show care without sounding performative.
Section 06
Do's & Don'ts
Specific phrases I use and phrases I never use. What makes an AI draft sound like me vs. like a template.
Section 07
Quick Reference Table
A compact lookup: scenario → tone → approach. For AI to use before drafting any email on my behalf.
Section 08
Calibration Tests
Three test prompts with expected responses. A way to verify the guide is working before using it at scale.

A shareable one-pager comparing Duke, UNC, and UT Austin on tuition cost vs. 10-year median earnings — built from the US Department of Education College Scorecard dataset. Documentation
AI-Powered Data Analysis of Duke, UT Austin, and UNC
The Workflow — Data to One-Pager
01
Started with a real dataset
Pulled the US Department of Education College Scorecard dataset — fields like in-state tuition, 10-year median earnings, acceptance rate, and enrollment. Filtered it down to Duke, UNC, and UT Austin plus a national average benchmark. Uploaded the CSV to Claude and asked for an initial read: what patterns are worth surfacing, and what a non-analyst would actually find useful.
02
Used AI to find the story in the data
Rather than writing formulas manually, I prompted Claude to analyze the dataset and flag the most meaningful comparison. It landed on cost vs. 10-year earnings — and surfaced the headline: UNC ($8,994/yr) and UT Austin ($11,688/yr) produce $72K–$75K in median earnings, almost matching Duke's $97K, at roughly one-sixth the tuition. That became the central argument of the one-pager.
03
Explored the data in Excel
Took the AI's recommended breakdown into a spreadsheet. Organized tuition and earnings side-by-side for all four data points (Duke, UNC, UT Austin, National Avg), cleaned up formatting, and used it as the source of truth for the chart values. This step locked in the exact numbers before any design work started.
04
Applied design skills to produce the one-pager
Used the /abc-design skill to generate a styled HTML one-pager. Gave Claude the three stat callouts (tuition per school), the chart data (earnings vs. tuition by school), the headline narrative, and the school color system — navy for Duke, Carolina blue for UNC, burnt orange for UT Austin. It produced a fully formatted, single-file HTML document with a grouped bar chart via Chart.js.
05
Made it shareable
Saved the finished HTML file to the project folder. Opens in any browser, no server required — just send the file. The whole pipeline, from raw CSV to polished one-pager, took under an hour. The output is the kind of thing you could drop into an email or share with a high schooler making a college decision.

How I Built It — Step by Step
01
Identified the recurring inefficiency
Every session ended with the same unresolved problem: what changed, what should be documented, and what manual steps could be automated? I was answering those questions by hand — or not at all. The pattern showed up twice. That was enough to build around it.
02
Defined the audit logic
The skill needed to do four things: read every SKILL.md file, scan scheduled tasks and memory files, check for gaps or broken references, and compare what was manually done in the session against what could be automated. I wrote the framework before writing any code — structured as four internal steps (delete, simplify, optimize, automate) borrowed from first-principles engineering.
03
Designed the output format first
The most important decision was the proposals table: four columns (Change, Target, Type, Why), one row per finding, status locked at "unapproved" until the user explicitly says go. No automatic changes. The table is the durable output — it compounds across sessions even when you don't approve everything immediately.
04
Used /skill-creator to package it
Packaged the audit logic into a structured SKILL.md using the skill creator. The file defines the trigger description, a short-session check (skip if nothing worth auditing), four internal audit steps, and three outputs: what was learned, the proposals table, and direct feedback on how to use Claude more efficiently. The skill reads the actual file system — not just the conversation.
05
Ran the first real audit — found 6 issues in under 2 minutes
The first live run of /learn read every skill, file, and scheduled task in the workspace and flagged 6 issues that had been silently failing for weeks: missing reference files, a disconnected review UI, a broken font path, and a missing CLAUDE.md that was causing me to re-explain context on every session. None of these were visible without the audit.

How I Built It — Step by Step
01
Mapped the manual process I was repeating every time
Before every intro call, I was doing the same 20-minute routine: pull up their LinkedIn, take notes on their background, figure out what package fits, write down what I wanted to ask. Same steps, every prospect. That's the pattern that tells you something should be automated.
02
Defined the outputs before writing any logic
The two things I actually needed were: (1) a structured Notion page I could open during the call, and (2) a concise brief I could read in 30 seconds before dialing. I wrote those output formats explicitly before building anything — that kept the skill from becoming a generic AI summary tool.
03
Built the diagnostic framework from my actual coaching logic
The gap analysis (materials · visibility · narrative), the blocker priority list, and the package recommendation logic aren't generic — they mirror how I actually think through a new prospect. 04

05
Packaged everything into a single skill with /skill-creator
